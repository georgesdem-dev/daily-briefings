🪖 Iran War — Day 69: Ceasefire Talks Stall; Oil Roars Back to $110

The defining market driver today is the reversal of ceasefire optimism. After Tuesday's 4.2% oil plunge on AFP ceasefire reports, Trump this morning poured cold water on a second round of direct negotiations: "It is too soon to consider further face-to-face talks," warning that if Tehran fails to comply with agreed terms, "the bombing starts, and it will be at a much higher level and intensity than before."

WTI crude spiked +8.3% to $110.40, Brent to $114.50. The Strait of Hormuz remains quasi-closed (under 12 vessels daily vs. the normal ~40). Iran's Supreme Leader dismissed Trump's latest timeline as "theatrical negotiation." Regional officials in Pakistan, which has been mediating, say talks are in "recess but not collapsed." Markets are pricing in a 40-60% probability of a deal within 30 days — down from 65% on Tuesday.

The key dynamic: every $10/bbl oil move adds roughly 30–40 bps to US CPI over 60 days, which directly complicates the Fed's already hawkish tilt under incoming Chair Kevin Warsh. The FOMC held rates at 3.5–3.75% at the April 29 meeting (8-4 split) — the widest dissent since October 1992. A sustained oil spike above $115 could delay any 2026 rate cuts entirely.

🏛️ Kevin Warsh Becomes Fed Chair May 15 — "Higher for Longer" Officially Locked In

With Jerome Powell's tenure ending and Kevin Warsh's appointment officially commencing May 15, markets are rapidly repricing the rate path. Warsh is described by insiders as a "price stability fundamentalist" — he dissented in favor of rate hikes during the post-2008 recovery when colleagues wanted accommodation, and he has consistently criticized the Fed for being too slow to fight inflation in 2021–2022. Gary Cohn calls him "the most hawkish credible candidate in a generation."

What this means for portfolios: (1) Bond prices down, yields up — 10-year at 4.38% today, could reach 4.6–4.8% by Q3 if oil stays elevated; (2) Rate-sensitive sectors hurt — utilities, REITs, and clean energy face multiple compression; (3) Banks and quality value benefit — JPM, BRK-B, and financials with strong balance sheets can pass higher rates to customers; (4) Cash and short-duration assets earn more — Berkshire's $397B cash hoard is now generating meaningful income at 4%+.

🧠 Trusted Investor Insights MACRO VIEWS
Warren Buffett / Greg Abel — Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting (May 2, 2026)

"A Church with a Casino Attached" — $397B Cash Signals Patience, Not Pessimism

In his first annual meeting as CEO, Greg Abel revealed Berkshire is holding $397 billion in cash and short-term Treasuries — an all-time record representing roughly one-third of its $1.2T market cap. Buffett, stepping back from the lectern but still philosophically engaged, described today's markets as "a church with a casino attached" — distinguishing between investors who hold businesses for years and those trading one-day options. "We've never had people in a more gambling mood than now," he said. Neither Buffett nor Abel is predicting a crash, but their capital allocation discipline signals they see insufficient margin of safety at current S&P valuations of 7,300+. Abel added Berkshire is thinking critically about AI applications but "won't do AI for the sake of AI." The implication for us: BRK-B below $474 is an accumulation opportunity — you're buying the world's best capital allocator at a meaningful discount to its sum-of-parts value, with $397B in dry powder for the next dislocation.

Howard Marks / Oaktree Capital — "We Are Not in a Safe Market"

Selectivity Over Breadth — The Warsh Fed Validates Marks' Caution

Howard Marks' recent memo takes on new urgency with Kevin Warsh replacing Powell. Marks argued that with S&P at 7,200+ and VIX at 17 (it's now back to 18.8), investors were pricing an optimistic scenario requiring everything to go right: Iran resolution, soft landing, AI earnings, and Fed cuts. With Warsh confirmed as a "price stability fundamentalist," the Fed cut scenario is now the weakest of those four legs. Marks' prescription: increase allocation to income-generating assets (corporate bonds, dividend stocks like JNJ, XOM, VIG), reduce high-beta growth names trading at 100x+ earnings, and maintain 15–20% in alternatives or cash for optionality. This brief's portfolio allocation is calibrated precisely to Marks' framework — AI infrastructure for growth, energy and healthcare for income and defense, BRK-B and SPY for diversified quality.
