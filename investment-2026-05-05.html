🪖 Iran War — Day 67: Markets Cheer Ceasefire Signals

Today's most significant macro driver: oil fell sharply (-4.2% to $101.95/bbl) on AFP reports that US–Iran ceasefire talks are showing progress, with a potential framework deal entering a drafting phase. The Strait of Hormuz remains quasi-closed (under 10 vessels transiting daily vs. the normal ~40), but futures traders are pricing in partial reopening within 30–60 days.

The dual dynamic of falling oil + rising equities is the market's clearest signal yet that investors believe the conflict will de-escalate without a nuclear dimension. Defense stocks (RTX, LMT, NOC) have given back some gains on this thesis. Energy stocks are mixed — higher production forecasts temper the commodity gain while integrated majors benefit from elevated prices today.

Jay Pelosky of TPW Advisory argues the Iran conflict could paradoxically unlock a "$10 trillion supercycle" — the forced acceleration of AI-driven defense tech, climate resilience infrastructure, and regional energy independence creating a once-in-a-decade spending wave. This thesis supports industrials, defense tech, and clean energy exposure.

Howard Marks / Oaktree Capital — Latest Thinking

"We Are Not in a Safe Market" — Selectivity Over Breadth

Oaktree's Howard Marks, in his most recent memo, cautions that with the S&P at 7,200+ and VIX at 17 (its lowest since January), investors are pricing in an optimistic scenario that requires everything to go right: Iran resolution, soft landing, AI earnings growth, and Fed cuts. His prescription for sophisticated investors: increase allocation to income-generating assets (corporate bonds, dividend stocks), reduce high-beta growth names that trade at 100x+ earnings, and maintain 15–20% in alternatives or cash for optionality. This brief's moderate-risk selections are designed to reflect Marks' framework — quality, income, and reasonable valuations.
