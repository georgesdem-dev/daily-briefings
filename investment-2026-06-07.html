💥 Friday's $1 Trillion Semiconductor Wipeout

Semiconductor Bloodbath: Broadcom (AVGO) dropped -7.9% after reporting weak Q3 chip revenue guidance — the shock spreading across the entire sector. Micron (MU) was the worst performer at -13.3% as memory chip crisis concerns resurfaced. AMD fell -10.9%, Intel -11.3%, Marvell (MRVL) -16%, and Nvidia (NVDA) -6.2%. The aggregate wipeout across chipmakers exceeded $1 trillion in market cap in a single session — the worst sector day since April 2025.

The Trigger: May nonfarm payrolls came in at a stunning +172,000 — more than double the ~80,000 consensus expectation. The blowout print immediately destroyed the rate-cut narrative: futures markets now price a Fed rate hike probability above 60% by year-end. High-multiple tech names, which had been priced for 2-3 cuts in 2026, were revalued in real time.

What It Means Going Forward: The S&P 500 had just set a new all-time high above 7,600 on Monday June 2 — that record now looks fragile. The 9-week winning streak is broken. However, fundamentals remain solid: Goldman Sachs maintains its 8,000 year-end target, noting the AI earnings cycle is still intact. The key question is whether this is a healthy correction or the start of a more sustained repricing.

🛢️ Oil Slides to $91 as Iran Nuclear Talks Advance

WTI crude fell -3.1% to $91/barrel on Friday — a deflationary signal driven by reports of meaningful progress in US-Iran nuclear negotiations. The Strait of Hormuz, which has been operating at reduced capacity since the conflict began, could see gradual reopening if a framework deal materializes. Energy stocks suffered a double blow: the sector fell alongside the broader market while oil itself provided no cushion.

Gold also fell -3.1% to $4,365/oz — its lowest level in weeks — as the strong dollar (fueled by the jobs report and yield surge) pressured precious metals. Ray Dalio's thesis of maintaining 10-15% gold as an inflation hedge has taken a short-term mark-to-market hit, but the long-term case for gold as a portfolio diversifier remains intact given global debt dynamics.

The 10-year Treasury yield spiked above 4.55% (30-year crossed 5%), levels that have historically served as a valuation ceiling for high-P/E tech stocks. This is the key macro variable to watch this week: if yields stabilize or retreat, the sell-off may find a floor. If they continue higher, expect further multiple compression in growth names.

🧠 Trusted Investor Insights MACRO VIEWS
Warren Buffett / Berkshire Hathaway — Q1 2026 13-F Filing

Rotating to Alphabet, Airlines, Homebuilders — Trimming Energy & Banks

Berkshire's Q1 2026 portfolio update reveals Buffett's big strategic pivot: massive new positions in Alphabet Class A (+204%) and Class C, Delta Air Lines (new position), and Lennar Corp (+43%). He trimmed Bank of America and slashed Chevron by 35%. Reading the tea leaves: Buffett is rotating from traditional value into quality growth (Alphabet) and cyclicals that benefit from a soft landing (airlines, homebuilders). His top holdings remain Apple (22% of portfolio), American Express (17%), Coca-Cola (12%), and Bank of America (9.5%). The Alphabet bet is particularly striking — it suggests Buffett sees AI-driven search and cloud as undervalued despite the stock's significant run. The cash pile (~$330B) remains as optionality for a major acquisition.

Goldman Sachs Macro Desk — June 2026

S&P 500 Year-End Target: 8,000 — "Market Still Has Room to Run"

Goldman Sachs raised its S&P 500 year-end 2026 target to 8,000 — representing roughly 8.4% further upside from Friday's close of 7,384. Goldman's thesis is that despite narrow market leadership concentrated in AI and energy, the underlying earnings cycle remains intact. Their top conviction additions for June include TPG (private equity, $61 PT, 43% upside), Casella Waste Systems (CWST, sustainable growth thesis), and Duke Energy (DUK, AI electricity demand). Goldman warns that the jobs report's impact on rate expectations is a near-term headwind, but argues the fundamental bull case — AI capex driving tech earnings above consensus — hasn't changed. Key risk: sticky inflation forcing a Fed hike rather than cut.

Ray Dalio / Bridgewater — Debt & Diversification Warning

"Very Dark Times Possible" — Build 15+ Uncorrelated Return Streams

Ray Dalio is sounding his most urgent alarm yet: equity risk premiums and credit spreads leave little margin of safety, while rising interest rate supply/demand pressures — driven by the US debt burden — could trigger a sharp market repricing. His prescription: maintain 10–15% gold allocation as a portfolio diversifier, allocate 10–15% to inflation-indexed bonds (offering ~2% real return as the "safest asset"), and build a portfolio of 15+ uncorrelated return streams to reduce risk by approximately 80%. He explicitly warns against the "very dark times" scenario: a debt crisis forcing a central bank choice between inflation and depression. The strong jobs report and yield spike this week is precisely the environment Dalio has been warning about.

Howard Marks / Oaktree Capital — June 2026 Letter

"Not Cheap — But Cheaper Than They Were" — Focus on What You Pay

Howard Marks's latest thinking: "The S&P is not cheap by historical standards — but cheaper than they were, which should be encouraging." His core principle — "it's not what you buy, it's what you pay that matters" — is directly applicable after Friday's selloff. Marks is focused on exploiting market mispricings and investor mistakes, and is bullish on credit market dispersion creating opportunities. He cautions against overly enthusiastic AI valuations and prefers income-generating assets where the return is contractual rather than speculative. The selloff creates exactly the kind of mispricing Marks exploits: quality income names (VZ, BMY, DUK) sold off alongside speculative chips — that's your opportunity.

JPMorgan Markets — June 2026 Mid-Year Outlook

Recession Risk at 35% — Still a Soft Landing Baseline, But Risks Rising

JPMorgan puts recession probability at approximately 35% — elevated but not the base case. Their mid-year outlook acknowledges that the strong jobs report is a double-edged sword: good for the economy but bad for rate-sensitive valuations. JPMorgan's focus list for June highlights AT&T (T, Buy, 4.52% yield), Entergy (ETR, Buy, utility focus), and Annaly Capital (NLY, 13% yield). Their key message: in a higher-for-longer world, income quality beats growth speculation. They maintain that US equities remain the preferred asset class globally, with international diversification as a tactical hedge. The critical data point to watch this week: CPI on June 11 — if inflation remains sticky, the rate-hike narrative will intensify.
