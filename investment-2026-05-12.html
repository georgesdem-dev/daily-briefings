📅 This Week's Critical Catalysts — Buckle Up

Tomorrow (Wed May 13) — CPI Print: The single biggest near-term market risk. Corporate executives are surveying inflation expectations at 3.7% annually. A reading above 3.5% YoY would effectively kill any Fed rate-cut hope for 2026, pressuring growth stocks and rate-sensitive sectors (REITs, utilities, tech). A tame number below 3.2% would be the catalyst for a sharp rally. The current 10-year yield at 4.41% — its highest level since March — signals the bond market is already bracing for a hot print.

Thursday–Friday (May 14–15) — Trump-Xi Summit: A potential trade framework announcement between the US and China could be a major positive catalyst for industrials, materials, and EM-exposed names. Conversely, any breakdown — particularly around Taiwan arms sales — creates semiconductor supply chain risk (TSMC, AMAT, ASML, NVDA). Watch this closely.

May 20 — NVIDIA Earnings: The single most important stock-specific event of Q2 2026. FY2027 revenue consensus is $374B (+73% YoY). Any beat-and-raise — the most likely scenario given hyperscaler AI capex commitments — would re-rate the entire AI stack (MSFT, AMD, MU). A miss, while unlikely, would be a systemic tech de-rating event. Position before May 16 to avoid pre-earnings volatility compression.

🪖 Iran War — Day 74: Oil Rebounds, Hormuz Risk Elevated

A week after markets rallied on ceasefire optimism (oil fell -4.2% on May 5), WTI crude has rebounded sharply to $101.15 (+3.14% today) as those early ceasefire signals have not materialized into a deal. The Strait of Hormuz remains under partial closure (~10 vessels/day vs. the pre-conflict ~40). Energy sector benefiting directly — XLE up +2.64% today. The Iran conflict has now entered Day 74 with no clear resolution timeline, creating a persistent geopolitical premium in energy prices that is structurally inflationary.

Key watch: Iran nuclear talks are the wildcard. Any announcement of weapons-grade enrichment resumption would send VIX above 30 and oil to $115+. Conversely, a real ceasefire framework deal could send oil to $85–90 and trigger a massive tech/consumer rotation. Hedging energy exposure while maintaining core defensive names (JNJ, KO, PG) is the moderate-risk playbook here.

📉 Macro Backdrop: Yields Rising, Buffett-Indicator Elevated, Fed on Hold

The 10-year Treasury yield is back at 4.41% — rising +1.05% today — reinforcing that the Fed is nowhere near cutting rates. Goldman Sachs has pushed its first-cut forecast out to at least 2027. Chicago Fed President Goolsbee said explicitly this week: "Inflation alone is getting worse — it's not stalled in its progress toward the target." With WTI at $101, that assessment rings true.

The "Buffett Indicator" (total market cap / GDP) remains historically elevated, consistent with Berkshire's near-record $330B+ cash hoard — Buffett is implicitly saying stocks are not cheap. Yardeni Research counters with an S&P year-end target of 8,250, predicting an earnings-led melt-up driven by AI productivity. HSBC sees 7,650. The tension between these bull and cautious views defines today's tape perfectly — markets grinding higher on earnings power but with growing anxiety on inflation and geopolitics.

Howard Marks / Oaktree Capital

"Sea Change" Thesis Playing Out — Selectivity Is Everything Now

Howard Marks continues to argue that the "sea change" in interest rates — the end of the 40-year era of declining rates — is the defining investment context of this decade. With 10-year yields at 4.41% and the Fed's next move likely being a hike rather than a cut (per some hawkish views), his advice is clear: increase allocation to income-generating assets (corporate bonds, quality dividend stocks like KO, PG, ABBV), reduce high-beta growth names trading at 100x+ earnings, and maintain 10–15% cash for optionality. The moderate-risk picks in this brief are designed to embody the Marks framework: quality, income, and valuation discipline.

Ray Dalio / Bridgewater — Stagflation Watch

Iran Conflict Validates Stagflation Risk Thesis

Bridgewater's known framework emphasizes the debt supercycle risk and geopolitical regime change. Dalio has repeatedly flagged US deficit spending and geopolitical conflict as long-term structural risks for US assets. The Iran conflict — now in Day 74 with no resolution and WTI back at $101 — is precisely the stagflation scenario Dalio's "changing world order" narrative predicted: supply-side inflation meets slowing consumer demand. His prescription: diversify globally, hold real assets (gold is near $4,708), and reduce duration risk (long bonds). The gold level itself is the market's stagflation signal — institutional money paying $4,700+ for gold means sophisticated investors are hedging a scenario where neither stocks nor bonds protect.
