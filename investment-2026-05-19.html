📈 10Y Treasury at 4.60% — The Annual High That Changes Everything

The most significant macro event of the week: the US 10-year Treasury yield traded at 4.60%–4.63% on May 19, its highest level in over a year. The proximate causes are threefold: (1) Elevated oil above $107/bbl from the Iran war keeps inflation expectations sticky; (2) Fed Chair speculation — Kevin Warsh, viewed as a "price stability fundamentalist," is the frontrunner to replace Powell, signaling no rate cuts on the horizon; (3) Record US Treasury auction supply in Q2 with weakening foreign demand is pushing yields higher structurally.

The investment implication is direct: growth stocks with high P/E ratios get compressed as the discount rate rises. This is precisely why Nasdaq fell for a second day while the Dow rose. For moderate-risk portfolios, this environment is a gift — it rewards dividend stocks, financials (higher NII), energy (real assets), and defensive blue chips with pricing power. All of today's top picks are positioned to benefit from, or at minimum weather, a 4.6%+ 10Y environment.

The silver lining: VIX eased to 17.82 (-3.31%) despite the yield surge, suggesting this is an orderly repricing rather than a fear-driven selloff. Investors are rotating, not fleeing. That is a bullish signal for portfolio construction.

🪖 Iran War — Day 81: Diplomacy Stalls, Oil Stays Elevated

Day 81 of the Iran conflict brings little change on the ground. The Strait of Hormuz remains restricted — shipping traffic is running at roughly 30% of pre-conflict norms, sustaining the WTI/Brent premium versus historical levels. US and Iranian negotiating teams are still in indirect talks via Omani intermediaries, but no ceasefire framework is imminent.

The market is treating the conflict as a sustained, low-grade geopolitical premium rather than an escalating crisis — hence the VIX at 17.82 rather than 25+. Energy companies are the primary beneficiaries. XOM, CVX, and the XLE ETF have built extraordinary free cash flow buffers at $100+ oil. Even if a ceasefire were announced tomorrow, the production ramp-up timeline (6–12 months for full Hormuz normalization) means energy FCF remains elevated through year-end. Energy remains the single best geopolitical hedge in a moderate-risk portfolio.

🧠 Trusted Investor Insights MACRO VIEWS
Warren Buffett / Berkshire Hathaway — Berkshire AGM, May 2026

"We've Never Had People in a More Gambling Mood Than Now"

At the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting in Omaha earlier this month, Buffett — now chairman emeritus — delivered his starkest warning yet about market speculation. He called out one-day options, prediction markets, and meme stocks as behaviors that have "crossed the line from investing into outright gambling." Berkshire's response: hold $397.4 billion in cash and T-bills, earning approximately $17.8 billion per year at 4.5% T-bill rates — more than many S&P 500 companies earn in total. The waiting strategy has historically been Buffett's most powerful move: he deployed aggressively in 2008, 2011, and 2020 after others panicked. At S&P 7,403 and a forward P/E of ~21x (vs. 16x historical average), he needs genuine fear, not rate-driven rotation, before pulling the trigger. Watch BRK-B below $460 as a signal that the opportunity is near.

Howard Marks / Oaktree Capital — "You Can't Predict. You Can Prepare."

Preparation Over Prediction: The Case for Income and Quality

Howard Marks' latest Oaktree memo carries a simple but profound message: in an environment where the 10Y Treasury yields 4.6%, the risk/reward calculus for high-multiple equities has fundamentally changed. Why accept a 2–3% earnings yield (50x P/E) from a speculative growth name when you can earn 4.6% from risk-free Treasuries or 8–10% from investment-grade corporate bonds? Marks' prescription: (1) increase allocation to income-generating assets — dividend stocks, REITs, IG credit; (2) ruthlessly reduce positions in companies trading at 100x+ earnings with no near-term path to profitability; (3) maintain 10–15% dry powder in cash or cash equivalents for the inevitable dislocation. This brief's portfolio — MSFT, JNJ, CVX, BRK-B, JPM, NEE — is designed to survive a Marks-style repricing event while still participating in upside. The gambling is being done by others.

Ray Dalio / Bridgewater Associates

"The Debt Cycle Is Entering a Critical Phase" — Diversify Into Hard Assets

Ray Dalio's public commentary in May 2026 continues to center on the structural debt burden facing the US. With the 10Y at 4.60% and federal deficits running at 6–7% of GDP, the interest cost on US debt is now consuming an ever-larger share of federal revenues — a dynamic Dalio has called "the big squeeze." His prescription for investors: diversify away from dollar-denominated paper assets and toward real assets — gold (currently $4,548/oz is Dalio's thesis in action), commodities, foreign equities, and inflation-protected securities (TIPS). For a moderate-risk portfolio, this argues for maintaining the CVX and NEE positions as "real asset" proxies, holding a 5–10% gold allocation (GLD ETF), and being selective about which bonds you hold. Dalio does not recommend avoiding equities — he recommends avoiding the naive assumption that the 2021–2025 bull market was "normal."
